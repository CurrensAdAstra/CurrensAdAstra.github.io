# AI, Easter Island, and the Last Tree

Are Easter Island's Moai statues truly vestiges of the Inca civilization? The truth about Rapa Nui revealed by genetics.
https://youtu.be/i4VvgT-nq2I?si=8mLPEyvmbQVuWR0O

The operator of that channel tends to dive deeply into personal research and sometimes ends up stepping a bit too far beyond the evidence.  
So let me add one more remark.

---

The tragedy of Easter Island remains one of the most painful examples of **ecological self-destruction** in human history.

In order to build the giant stone statues known as *Moai*, the island’s tribes cut down tree after tree—eventually the very last one. The warning signs were there: when forests disappear, civilizations collapse. Yet the tribes ignored them. What mattered more was competition. Each group wanted to erect statues larger and more magnificent than those of their rivals.

As the number of trees declined, their value increased. But no one could say, “Let us be the first to stop.”  
The fear was simple: if we stop, the neighboring tribe will surpass us.  
That fear accelerated the path toward mutual destruction.

---

This tragic landscape looks eerily similar to today's **global AI arms race** unfolding in the middle of the climate crisis.

Massive data centers consume enormous amounts of electricity and cooling water while emitting large quantities of carbon. Yet global technology companies continue accelerating. Increasing the parameter count of large language models (LLMs) is often treated as if it were equivalent to possessing the nuclear weapons of the future.

In that logic, the urgent issue of global warming quietly moves to the background.

The same competitive obsession that drove Easter Island tribes to carve ever larger statues seems to be reappearing today—only this time the statues are enormous neural networks.

---

Even more paradoxical is our **technological optimism**.

As the environment deteriorated, the priests of Easter Island reportedly responded by building even larger statues, hoping for divine intervention.

Modern society does something strikingly similar.

We destroy parts of the environment to build massive AI systems—and then comfort ourselves with the idea that **AI will eventually solve the climate crisis**.

We are placing our hopes for salvation in the very tool that contributes to the problem.  
In essence, this is not so different from a civilization desperately erecting its final monuments while collapse approaches.

---

Jared Diamond once asked a haunting question:

> *What was the person thinking who cut down the last tree on Easter Island?*

Perhaps he said something like:

- "It's my job."  
- "If I don't cut it down, someone from the neighboring village will."  
- "Surely the priests will find a solution."

Today we make similar excuses.

The language may sound more sophisticated—*industrial necessity*, *technological progress*, *innovation*.  
But these phrases often serve the same purpose: they justify the final swing of the axe.

---

In the end, the people of Easter Island destroyed their forests to the point that they could no longer even build canoes to escape the island. They remained trapped on a barren land of their own making.

We, too, live on an isolated island.

It is called **Earth**.

If we succeed in building magnificent AI systems but destroy the environment that sustains us, those technological achievements may ultimately mean very little.

(One might even say this explains why Elon Musk seems so eager to go to Mars.)

---

Perhaps one day humanity will curse AI.

Or perhaps we already are.

---

And I should admit something.

I may very well be one of the people making those excuses.
